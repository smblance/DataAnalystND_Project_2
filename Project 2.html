Section 1. Statistical Test¶

As the data is not normally distributed and unpaired (see Section 3), we will use the Mann-Whitney test to check whether the rain will positively affect the hourly entries.
The null hypothesis will be: "The medians of the distributions of the hourly entries with rain and without rain are the same."
The alternative hypothesis: "The median of the distribution of the hourly entries with rain is higher than the median of the distribution of the entries without rain."

The means are 2028.20 hourly entries when it rains, and 1845.54 entries when it doesn't.
The (left-tailed) critical value for 1% significance level with sample sizes of n1 = 9585 and n2 = 33064 is 155990241.5.
As the value of the statistic is less than this critical value, we reject the null hypothesis.
The obtained p-value is 2.74×10-6.
Thus, we can conclude that people use the subway more when it rains.

Section 2. Linear Regression¶

For making the linear regression, we will use the OLS approach, as implemented in Scikit-Learn package.
As features we will use:

  • Weather variables: 'conds', 'fog', 'rain', 'precipi', 'pressurei', 'tempi', 'wspdi'
  • General variables: 'UNIT', 'day_week', 'hour'

The features 'conds', 'UNIT', 'day_week', 'hour' are used as dummy features, because they are categorical.
These features were chosen as they give an increase of R2 value.
The other possible features are strongly connected with the chosen features and thus were not included in the model.

The general variables are giving the most of the R2, because the difference between entries on the busy and quiet stations/hours can hardly be offset by weather conditions.
Also, there are two factors to support this:

  • the weather conditions do not vary much between stations at any given time
  • we are using weather stations for estimating the weather conditions at the subway stations, thus homogenizing weather between stations even more.

Section 4. Conclusion¶

With the Mann-Whitney test we have confirmed that the effect of rain on the hourly entries is significant and positive.
Although the coefficient for the rain feature in the linear regression is negative, it's also very small: -5 compared to -885 for fog. Also we've established that the linear regression does not describe the relationship very well.

Section 5. Reflection¶

The main shortcoming of the data is that the weather variables are not evaluated precisely at the time and location of the turnstile unit.
Mainly, if we want to evaluate the effect of rain on other variables, we may want to know if it's raining at the exact time at which we measure the traffic. The variable 'rain', which indicates if rain occurred within the calendar day at the location, is only an estimate of that.
Also, if our goal is to predict the future traffic by weather, we may want to base our analysis on the weather forecast, which, generally speaking, predicts the weather with only a certain accuracy.
The linear regression does not describe the relationship very well, so if we want to capture the relationship between weather and subway traffic, we need to employ a more sophisticated model.
Nevertheless, we have confirmed that rain positively affects the subway traffic, and when we expect heavy raining, the measures for handling increased traffic must be taken.
